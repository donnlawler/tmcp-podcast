<h2 class="transcript-heading">Transcript</h2>

<div class="transcript-content">

<p>
This is The Minimum Commitment – Film Theory in Small Doses. Where the monsters don’t come for us—they come from us.
</p>

<p>
I’m Donn Lawler — film scholar, sound engineer, and someone who knows that the moment you stop recognizing yourself, you might already be replaced.
</p>

<p>
Let’s get into it.
</p>

<p>
Jordan Peele’s Us opens in the glow of nostalgia. A television hums in a dark living room, playing the Hands Across America commercial from 1986. Smiling faces, hands held tightly, a nation promising to heal itself through gesture. As the camera moves closer, the image fills the frame — a performance of unity that feels strangely hollow, a dream broadcast on repeat. Framing the screen, a row of VHS tapes tells a deeper story. To the left: C.H.U.D., the 80s horror film about discarded humans living underground. Beside it, The Goonies, a suburban fantasy where adventure waits below the surface. Then The Man with Two Brains—identity split in half, personality divided. On the right, the two-tape box set of The Right Stuff, a celebration of American greatness that excludes everyone outside the myth. These tapes act as the film’s subconscious, a coded summary of everything Jordan Peele is about to unpack—the buried, the forgotten, the invisible.
</p>

<p>
Freud called it the uncanny, that eerie sensation when something feels both familiar and foreign. He said the double arises when we repress a part of ourselves… and that part refuses to stay buried. Jung called it the Shadow. The pieces of us we deny. The parts we’re ashamed of. The truths we’re too afraid to look at head-on. In Us, the Tethered are that Shadow made flesh. They are not aliens. They are not monsters. They are us—split in half. Adelaide is the key. A girl once stolen, forced underground, who climbs her way back to the surface, only to discover she no longer belongs in either world. She is both victim and thief. Mother and monster. Hero and intruder. When Red—her mirror self—says, “We are Americans,” it isn’t a punchline. It’s a diagnosis. Because the horror of Us isn’t being hunted. It’s realizing you’re the one who set the trap.
</p>

<p>
Cut to Santa Cruz at night. The boardwalk glows with carnival light, an image of safety and leisure that feels just a little too bright. A family walks the midway—father, mother, daughter. The sounds of laughter, games, and waves all blend into a fragile calm. Young Adelaide, played with haunting stillness by Madison Curry, wanders off while her parents argue, drawn toward a small attraction set apart from the noise and lights. The sign above reads “Vision Quest,” a promise of spiritual adventure, and beneath it, glowing in carnival bulbs, the words “Find Yourself.” It’s the kind of slogan that never changes — a recycled message from every decade of American amusement. Each generation steps inside believing they’ll come out transformed, never realizing how easily transformation can become distortion. Inside, reflections multiply endlessly. The mirrors distort, repeat, and reshape until identity begins to blur. And in the quiet that follows, something familiar steps forward—a version of Adelaide that doesn’t obey the rules of reflection. Peele makes the reflection move on its own accord. The simplest cinematic gesture, and it changes everything.
</p>

<p>
This is how Us begins its argument. A TV commercial of false harmony, followed by a maze where recognition turns to fear. Peele builds the connection deliberately: America’s obsession with self-image leads straight to its reflection—and that reflection refuses to look away. When Peele returns to this world decades later, we meet adult Adelaide Wilson, now a mother played by Lupita Nyong’o. Her husband Gabe, played by Winston Duke, fills their home with easy humor and bluster. Their children, Zora, played by Shahadi Wright Joseph, and Jason, portrayed by Evan Alex, move through the story with a mix of innocence and awareness — two reflections of their mother’s growing unease. Nyong’o anchors the film. Her performance carries quiet panic, a tension that never left the funhouse. We read her fear as trauma. Peele frames it as memory. He shows how easily perception can shift, and how quickly the familiar can turn against you. The film’s surface is pure Americana—beaches, boardwalks, family trips, soundtracked by pop songs and laughter. Beneath that brightness runs something obscured. The tunnels mentioned in the prologue—stretching under cities, forgotten, and unacknowledged—wait like a buried conscience. The kind of history a country pretends not to remember. And from that silence, something begins to rise.
</p>

<p>
Jordan Peele builds his horror from reflection. Every frame of the film turns the familiar into something studied and precise. He doesn’t imagine a new threat. He reveals one already in motion. The film’s design rests on a single idea: for every surface, there is an underside. The people who live above are supported by those forced to remain unseen. The Tethered are the physical embodiment of that hierarchy — a population left behind, mirroring the actions of those who walk freely. They are imitation given flesh, condemned to repeat the gestures of others without reward.
</p>

<p>
This is the language of othering —  a powerful and often subtle process that constructs a sense of belonging by systematically excluding those who do not fit certain criteria.  It defines identity by drawing an invisible boundary,  actively deciding who is included inside and who is pushed outside.  The privileged classes sustain their control by propagating the illusion that such separation and hierarchy are natural and inevitable. Peele exposes this fabrication by creating an entire society beneath the floorboards,  making the hidden and distorted world of exclusion painfully visible. The world of Us operates as a class diagram. The surface is comfort, consumption, and denial. Below it lies labor, imitation, and suffering. When the Tethered rise, they are not invaders;  they are the bill come due. Their revolution is visibility. They appear only to remind the nation that its reflection has form. The tunnels beneath America are more than setting. They are collective memory — the parts of history buried for convenience. Forgotten experiments. Abandoned communities. Stories no one wrote down. Peele fills those corridors with motion and noise, a network of lost voices still moving in rhythm with the living above. Peele turns horror into social anatomy. He layers metaphor and form until they are one. Every mirror, every tunnel, every silent reflection is part of the same argument: the distance between privilege and despair is smaller than it appears, and the descent from one to the other takes only a few steps down.
</p>

<p>
Jordan Peele composes Us with mathematical precision. The images, the sound, and movements — all echo the film’s reflective design. He turns the language of film into a hall of mirrors — a space where form and meaning collapse into one another. Duality defines this world. The Tethered don’t just resemble the living; they imitate them. Their movements are a choreography of subjugation — each action above echoed by a distorted version below. What looks like horror is actually repetition, a life lived through mimicry, stripped of agency. The film’s structure follows the same pattern. Peele folds the story against itself, building scenes in pairs: the family above ground and the family beneath it, two realities sharing a single rhythm. He uses symmetry to question identity, then uses repetition to prove how fragile that identity truly is. Cinematographer Mike Gioulakis frames each scene with surgical precision. Horizontal lines divide screen space like class boundaries. Characters are placed dead center, their balance on screen matching the illusion of balance in life. Every composition feels perfect until something moves slightly out of sync — a smile that lasts too long, a reflection that doesn’t follow. Peele uses these fractures to erode trust in the image itself.
</p>

<p>
The downward motion repeats throughout the film. Escalators, basements, tunnels — each descent leads deeper into the architecture of denial. When Adelaide rides the escalator toward her Tethered doppelganger in the final act, it feels less like rescue and more like reckoning. The camera tracks her motion with deliberate slowness, each step downward erasing a layer of comfort. Peele turns vertical movement into moral language. Descending involves confronting what has been concealed. On a visual level, Peele builds meaning through repetition. The red jumpsuits of the Tethered flatten individuality — one color, one uniform, one shared history of neglect. Scissors, the chosen weapon, complete the symbol. Two identical halves joined by a single hinge. By the time the families meet in the Wilson living room, symmetry has consumed the frame. Each character faces their counterpart at equal distance, perfectly balanced along the camera's axis. But balance isn’t harmony—it’s tension. Peele traps his characters inside the geometry of their own denial. Every frame becomes a diagram of guilt.
</p>

<p>
Sound in Us doesn’t just accompany the image — it guides interpretation. Peele designs his soundtrack like a second script, one that speaks when language fails. The first cue sets the tone. A choral arrangement swells over the opening titles, rhythmic and ritualistic, somewhere between song of praise and warning. The music builds space before any image appears, inviting the audience to listen before they see. It’s the sound of something sacred becoming unfamiliar. Silence becomes just as expressive. Moments without dialogue are carved with care, forcing attention to the environment — the soft scrape of footsteps, the breath between words, the static hum of a tunnel light. This absence isn’t emptiness; it’s pressure. It’s the weight of everything the characters don’t say. Then comes the voice. Lupita Nyong’o performs both Adelaide and her Tethered doppelganger Red, with haunting contrast. Adelaide’s voice carries warmth, measured and calm even when she’s afraid. Red’s voice is ruin — breathy, broken, as if language itself had to claw its way up from the dark. It’s a sound that aches, the physical evidence of a life without air. Nyong’o built that vocal tone from the condition known as spasmodic dysphonia, a disorder that constricts speech and forces every word through strain. Her delivery transforms trauma into resonance. When she speaks, you hear the years she spent silenced. The Tethered communicate through rhythm and imitation rather than words. Grunts, gasps, laughter — sounds that echo humanity without language. Their voices exist like fossils of communication, the remnants of something once whole. It’s an auditory mirror of class and access: those above speak freely; those below make noise. Peele weaponizes that contrast in the home invasion sequence. The Wilsons try to reason, ask questions, establish control. The Tethered answer with silence, then with a symphony of breath and motion. Doors creak, glass shatters, footsteps syncopate against the floor. The scene feels musical, but the melody is fear. In the film, hearing is reckoning. The echoes are everywhere, and none of them fade.
</p>

<p>
Everything in Us leads to the final conflict. Adelaide descends into the tunnels beneath Santa Cruz, retracing the forgotten design of America’s underbelly. The journey feels like myth — a modern Orpheus descending not for love,
but for truth. The camera follows her down the escalator, its mechanical hum steady and cold. The light flickers, the air grows sterile. This isn’t a place for the living; it’s a museum for the abandoned. When Adelaide finally finds Red, Peele slows the rhythm. The confrontation unfolds like choreography — half dance, half duel. Lupita Nyong’o delivers a dual performance of astonishing precision. As Adelaide, she moves with human weight — heavy breathing, quick glances, instinctive fear. As Red, she moves with ritual grace — slow, deliberate, each step a punctuation mark. Their bodies communicate what words cannot. Every mirrored gesture collapses the distance between them, until they no longer feel like separate people but two halves of the same impulse: survival. When the fight ends, the camera lingers. Adelaide stands over Red, panting. The score falls silent. Only breathing remains — one steady, one fading. Red sucks in a final breath, and tries to whistle, a fractured echo of childhood — the “Itsy Bitsy Spider” returning one last time. Adelaide watches, eyes wide, and something shifts behind them. Recognition. She wraps the chain of her handcuffs around Red’s throat, pulling tight until the sound stops. And in that silence, memory surfaces. The night everything changed. Adelaide was never the victim. She was the one who walked out of the dark and never looked back. In that revelation, Peele completes his design. The monster we feared was the reflection we ignored. Identity, morality, and privilege collapse into a single question: what makes us who we are — birth? Circumstance? Survival?
</p>

<p>
The revelation isn’t meant to shock. It’s meant to expose how easily we mistake comfort for innocence. Adelaide didn’t actually escape anything. She just inherited a different life. Everything she has — the house, the calm, the sense of safety — came from someone else being forced to stay below. This isn’t judgment. Peele just wants us to see her clearly. The doppelgängers or the scissors or the tunnels are not the true horror of Us. It’s the unsettling truth that seeking comfort always comes at a price paid by someone else. The fragile balance we rely on isn’t genuine harmony; It’s a delicate system rooted in superficial reflection rather than authentic empathy. Outside, the Tethered stand hand in hand across the country, their red uniforms stretching beyond the horizon. It’s the old Hands Across America dream reborn as nightmare, the parody of unity replacing the real thing. Peele’s message is quiet, but unrelenting: you can’t destroy your reflection without losing yourself. Every act of denial deepens the divide. Every privilege left unexamined becomes another tether waiting to pull tight. Jordan Peele’s Us holds the mirror steady until the audience looks away. He isn’t chasing fear of the unknown; he’s exposing the terror of recognition — the moment you realize the danger doesn’t live outside your home. It’s been staring back in every reflection you’ve ever trusted.
</p>

<p>
That’s your thought for the day—minimum commitment, maximum meaning. See you next time.
</p>
