<h2 class="transcript-heading">Transcript</h2>

<div class="transcript-content">

<p>
This is The Minimum Commitment: Film Theory in Small Doses.
Where the scariest part of turning to the dark side
is that it starts - in your head.   
</p>

<p>
I’m Donn Lawler—film scholar, Sonic purist,
and someone who can’t unsee Anakin Skywalker as a walking contradiction.
</p>

<p>
Let’s get into it.
</p>

<p>
In Revenge of the Sith, the galaxy collapses into empire
but the real destruction happens inside one man. 
Anakin Skywalker doesn’t fall all at once.
He’s unraveled.
And slowly pulled apart. 
And if you look at his journey through a psychoanalytic lens
you’ll see that it’s not just a moral failure.
It’s an identity crisis.
A war between desire and repression. 
</p>

<p>
Freud argued that repressed desires govern the unconscious
especially fear of abandonment, fear of death,
and the impossible urge to control what we cannot. 
Anakin embodies that.
He loses his mother in Attack of the Clones and now, 
haunted by dreams of his wife Padmé dying, he spirals into panic. 
He wants to stop death itself. To master it.
To bend the natural order to his will. 
Not out of ambition but out of fear.
And that’s where Palpatine steps in.
Not as a tyrant, but as a false oracle
whispering promises of forbidden knowledge. 
He doesn’t offer power.
He offers dominion over the very structure of loss. 
That’s the key.
And that’s the trap. 
This isn’t a fall into evil.
It’s a slow unraveling.
A crawl into a new kind of madness shaped by love, fueled by fear, and sealed by silence. 
It’s not ambition that drives him.
It’s the unbearable idea that fate can’t be bargained with.
So he tries to rewrite it.
By any means necessary. 
</p>

<p>
Lacan expands Freud’s theory by saying we’re always chasing something we can’t fully name.
He calls it the unattainable object of desire. 
For Anakin, that object shifts.
First, it’s his mother.
Then, Padmé.
Then, the idea of control. 
But every time he reaches for it, he loses something else. 
His fear of loss becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.
The tighter he holds on, the more everything slips through his fingers. 
He wants to be a Jedi.
He wants to be a husband.
He wants to be a savior. 
But he’s been told, clearly and repeatedly, that he can’t be a Jedi, a husband, a father, and a protector.
Not all at once.
Not in their order.
Not under their laws. 
So he fractures.
Becomes two selves in one body.
The warrior and the lover.
The servant and the heretic.
And like all fallen heroes, he begins to believe the only way to save the world is to break it first. 
</p>

<p>
Let’s look at one moment.
Palpatine and Anakin in the opera house.
No lightsabers. No Force lightning. Just a story. 
The tale of Darth Plagueis the wise. 
Palpatine says:
“He had such a knowledge of the dark side, he could even keep the ones he cared about… from dying.” 
That line hits like a spell.
Anakin doesn’t ask how it works. He doesn’t care if it’s true. 
Because in that moment, Palpatine isn’t tempting him, he’s diagnosing him. 
Anakin doesn’t need more training. He needs therapy. 
His final transformation isn’t a leap into evil it’s a severing. A ritual sacrifice of the self.
He cuts away the parts that make him human:
Love.
Grief.
Hope.
Not because he wants to but because he believes he has to.
Because he’s been told that to save what he loves, he must become something else.
Something colder. Something unbreakable. 
So he kills his brothers.
He strangles the woman he swore to protect.
And he kneels, not to power, but to the only thing left that promises certainty. 
This isn’t a triumph. It’s a ritual burial.
The death of Anakin Skywalker, not by enemy hands, but by his own. 
</p>

<p>
He doesn’t put on the mask.
It’s built for him.
Bolted on.
Sealed tight.
A final act of control masquerading as salvation. 
What emerges isn’t power
it’s hollow grief sealed in black armor.
A scream turned into silence. 
Vader isn’t a symbol of dominance.
He’s a monument to what’s been lost.
The mask doesn’t protect him.
It preserves the ruin. 
</p>

<p>
Revenge of the Sith is a tragedy.
Not because of what Anakin becomes but because of what he couldn’t allow himself to feel. 
He didn’t lose himself.
He buried what was left.
Sealed it behind silence and steel. 
Because in this galaxy,
desire without understanding becomes fear.
And fear always, inevitably leads to ruin.  
</p>

<p>
That’s your thought for the day—minimum commitment, maximum meaning.
See you next time.
</p>
