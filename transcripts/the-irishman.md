<h2 class="transcript-heading">Transcript</h2>

<div class="transcript-content">

<p>
You’re listening to The Minimum Commitment: Film Theory in Small Doses.
Where we ask one big question through one small slice of cinema.
</p>

<p>
I’m Donn Lawler—film scholar, audio obsessive, and someone who keeps rewatching the last 30 minutes of The Irishman… because something in it still hurts.
</p>

<p>
Let’s get into it.
</p>

<p>
When Martin Scorsese released The Irishman in 2019, it wasn’t just another gangster film.
It was something else.
A coda.
A meditation.
A slow farewell.
But it was also something very modern:
a streaming-era experiment backed by Netflix to the tune of nearly $160 million.
That number matters.
Because it wasn’t Paramount who paid it.
It wasn’t Warner Bros. or Universal either.
They all passed. 
Scorsese took the film to Netflix  because they said yes.
They gave him full creative control, a massive budget, and cutting-edge digital de-aging tech. 
It was, in many ways, a filmmaker’s dream. 
But the results?
Complicated. 
Let’s be honest, Netflix needed The Irishman as much as Scorsese needed funding.
They wanted prestige, credibility, Oscar clout.
Something to prove they weren’t just an algorithm feeding you true crime docs and dating shows. 
Scorsese, in turn, wanted freedom
to make the film he envisioned without worrying about runtime, theatrical box office, or studio compromise. 
It was a gamble for both parties.
But especially for Netflix.
They bet $160 million on a 3.5 hour movie starring actors in their late seventies, exploring guilt, aging, and existential isolation. 
That’s not your typical streaming hit.
And for a lot of viewers, it wasn’t easy to finish in one sitting.
</p>

<p>
The Irishman doesn’t move like Goodfellas.
It doesn’t swing. 
It weighs. 
It slows you down. 
The violence is restrained and often shown from a distance.
The pacing is a deliberate crawl toward the inevitable. 
It’s Scorsese at his most reflective.
The story isn’t about rising up
it’s about looking back, and realizing there’s nowhere left to go. 
Frank Sheeran, played by Robert De Niro, isn’t a charismatic antihero.
He’s an empty vessel.
A man who followed orders  and now faces the silence that comes after. 
No redemption.
No glory.
Just a hospital bed, a funeral plot, and a daughter who won’t return his calls.
</p>

<p>
So what does The Irishman tell us about authorship today? 
On one hand, it’s pure Scorsese.
The cast. The tone. The Catholic guilt.
It’s unmistakably his.
But on the other hand, it only exists because Netflix exists. 
That raises a new question:
Is the modern auteur now dependent on the platforms that enable their vision? 
The Irishman wasn’t shaped by market research.
It wasn’t forced into a two-hour runtime.
It wasn’t built for multiplexes. 
But it also didn’t perform the way Netflix hoped.
It didn’t go viral.
It didn’t trend.
And it didn’t drive the kind of mass engagement a $160 million investment usually requires. 
Some called it a masterpiece.
Others called it a vanity project.
And Netflix? 
They haven’t funded anything quite like it since. 
</p>

<p>
The Irishman is ultimately a film about what it costs to follow the rules in a world that never cared about you. 
And in a weird way, that’s what this release experiment reflects also.
It followed the rules of prestige filmmaking—
but the world had moved on. 
We binge now.
We scroll.
We watch with one eye on our phones. 
Scorsese made a slow film for a fast world. 
And that dissonance? 
That’s part of the story. 
</p>

<p>
So maybe The Irishman wasn’t a failure.
Maybe it wasn’t a hit either. 
Maybe it was what it needed to be:
A quiet gamble.
A farewell letter.
A warning. 
Streaming may offer freedom
but freedom without audience is a kind of silence. 
</p>

<p>
That’s your thought for the day—minimum commitment, maximum meaning.
See you next time.
</p>
